An Example of Natural Selection
Giraffes have long necks.

Giraffes with slightly longer necks could feed on leaves of higher

branches when all lower ones had been eaten off.
 They had a better chance of survival.
 Favorable characteristics propagated through generations of
giraffes.
 Now, evolved species have long necks.
NOTE: Longer necks may have been a mutation initially but since it


trait.

GA Requirements
A typical genetic algorithm requires two things to be

defined:
◦ a genetic representation of the solution domain, and
◦ a fitness function to evaluate the solution domain.
The main property that makes these genetic

representations convenient is that their parts are easily
aligned due to their fixed size, that facilitates simple
crossover operation.
Variable length representations may also be used, but

crossover implementation is more complex in this case.
Tree-like representations are explored in Genetic

programming.

--- Page 11 ---
GA Requirements
The fitness function is defined over the genetic

representation and measures the quality of the
represented solution.
The fitness function is always problem dependent.

A representation of a solution might be an array of bits,

where each bit represents a different object, and the
value of the bit (0 or 1) represents whether or not the
object is available.

Genetic Algorithm (GA) Steps:
(1) Initialize a population (P) of n elements.
(Randomly generate solutions or "chromosomes" for the problem).
(2) Until a specified termination condition is satisfied (perform iterations).
(2 ) Determine the fitness for all the solutions in the population.
𝑎
(The fitness of a solution is a measure that can be used to compare solutions
to determine which is better. For example, a company is trying to maximize a
profit. The profit itself can be used as the fitness).
(2 ) (Selection) Creation of a mating pool, Use a fitness function to evaluate
𝑏
each element of the current population. If an element passes the fitness
criteria, it remains in P.
(2 ) (Crossover). Select randomly internal points (crossing sites) of the solutions,
𝑐
then swap the solution parts that follow these points. randomly selecting a
pair at a time.
(2 ) (mutation). randomly select a small portion of the solutions and artificially
𝑑
change it (e.g., a bit of 1 to 0 or 0 to 1).

--- Page 14 ---
Genetic Learning Operators:
◦ Apply an evolutionary approach to inductive learning
(based on Darwinian principle of natural selection).
◦ Genetic algorithm can be developed for supervised and
unsupervised data mining.
◦ Crossover: forms new elements from the population by
combining parts of two elements currently in the population.
◦ Mutation: can be applied by randomly flipping bits within a
single elements.

Limitations of Genetic Algorithms

◦ Does not guarantee an optimal solution (often settles
in a sub optimal solution / local minimum)
◦ Not all problems can be put into GA formulation
◦ Development and interpretation of GA solutions
requires both programming and statistical skills
◦ Relies heavily on the random number generators
◦ Locating good variables for a particular problem and
obtaining the data for the variables is difficult
◦ Selecting methods by which to evolve the system
requires experimentation and experience
